Abstract

Recent video generation models have achieved impressive visual fidelity, yet they often produce physically implausible motions, limiting their realism in dynamic scenes. This limitation stems from the fact that real-world object motion unfolds in three-dimensional space, while video observations provide only partial, view-dependent projections of such dynamics. Motivated by this observation, we propose PhysVideo, a physics-driven framework that models foreground motion across multiple views to better capture physically grounded dynamics, and subsequently synthesizes background-aware videos. PhysVideo follows a two-stage paradigm: in the first stage, Phys4View generates temporally coherent and geometrically consistent four-view videos conditioned on a single image, a text prompt, and explicit physical attributes, using physics-aware and spatiotemporal attention mechanisms; in the second stage, VideoSyn leverages the generated multi-view motion as guidance and learns data-driven interactions between foreground dynamics and background context for controllable video synthesis. To support training and evaluation, we construct PhysMV, the first large-scale physics-oriented video dataset, comprising 40K 4-view videos generated with a physics engine and annotated with physical attributes. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PhysVideo significantly improves physical realism and spatial-temporal coherence over existing video generation methods.
